✨ Preparation

Before you draw

A short, friendly walk-through to get you set up and ready — before your first real drawing lesson begins.

Welcome! This is your preparation lesson — a calm, friendly walk-through that gets you fully ready before you begin Lesson 1: The Cube.

Here we focus on the small things that make a big difference: your materials, your workspace, your hand position, and your first warm-up shapes. You don't need experience — just curiosity.

Why this comes first: The Cube lesson moves quickly into real drawing. If your pencil, posture, and paper are already sorted out, you can focus on the art — not the setup.

Part 2 · Get ready

Basic Preparation

Your materials, workspace, and setup before the pencil hits paper.

📦 What you'll need
  • ✏️
    Pencils — HB, 2B, 3B, 2H
    A range of softness lets you go light (2H) for sketching to dark (3B) for shadow work.
  • 📄
    Drawing paper
    Smooth or lightly textured sketch paper. A4 or letter size works well to start.
  • 🧽
    Eraser (kneaded + regular)
    Kneaded eraser for lifting, regular eraser for sharper corrections.
  • 🧻
    Blending tool (tissue or paper stump)
    Used to soften and unify tones — you'll see this in every lesson.
  • 🖼️
    Easel or angled surface (optional)
    Helps reduce perspective distortion. A simple desk works too.
  • 🩹
    Masking tape / artist tape (optional)
    For taping down your paper. See the optional video below.

🩹 Taping down your paper
🎯 Optional · Not required

Some artists like to tape their paper down before drawing — it keeps the page steady and creates clean edges. The teacher shows a full taping technique in this video.

🎬 Optional · Taping demo
You don't have to copy this exactly. You can tape only the four edges, or tape more completely like the teacher does. A more complete taping is usually used when the full artwork includes the background — that's why the teacher tapes more fully in this demo.
💡 For first-time drawers
If you're just doing the cube exercise, taping the four edges is plenty. Save the full taping setup for when you're building a complete artwork with background.
🧘 Posture & readiness

Before you start: sit comfortably, with your back relaxed and your drawing surface in front of you. Keep your arm free to move — drawing isn't just from the wrist, it's from the shoulder for big strokes and from the fingers for details.

Take a breath. The next 30 minutes are yours.

Part 3 · Loosen up

Hand Warm Up

How to hold your pencil + how to wake up your drawing hand.

👋 Wake up your hand

Before drawing, give your hand 30 seconds of attention:

  • Open and close your fist 10 times.
  • Wiggle each finger separately.
  • Roll your wrist slowly in both directions.
  • Hold your pencil and make 10 free squiggles on scratch paper.

This isn't optional — a stiff hand makes for stiff lines.

✋ How to hold the pencil

There isn't one correct grip — there are several that work well. Below are three hand positions we recommend. All of them are acceptable. Pick the one that feels natural and matches what you're drawing.

① Large-scale grip — recommended for big drawings ⭐ Featured
This grip is great for large-scale drawing. Hold the pencil farther from the tip — you'll have more arm freedom and lighter, sweeping strokes. Watch the distance between your hand and the pencil tip — that gap gives you control over your line weight.
② Left-handed version — acceptable ✅ OK
For left-handed students: this grip works well and feels natural. Same principles — keep enough distance between your hand and the pencil tip so you can see what you're drawing.
③ Right-handed version — acceptable ✅ OK
For right-handed students: a comfortable, close-to-the-tip grip for more precise control. Good for detailed work and smaller drawings.
💡 The key thing to notice
Pay attention to the distance between your hand grip and the pencil tip. Closer = more precision but stiffer lines. Farther = more freedom but less detail control. Different drawings call for different distances.
Part 4 · Practice

Simple Shapes

A few easy shape exercises to build line confidence.

Before drawing a cube, it helps to practice drawing the lines that make a cube — straight lines, controlled lengths, parallel pairs. These exercises take 5–10 minutes and are worth doing before every drawing session.

Part 5 · Bridge to Cube

Basic Perspective Awareness

The smallest amount of perspective theory you need before Cube.

You don't need to learn full perspective theory before the cube lesson. But here's the one idea worth knowing:

Things farther from your eye look smaller. Lines that go away from you appear to converge. That's it. The cube lesson will show you how to use this in practice — for now, just keep this idea in the back of your mind.

💡 Notice it everywhere
Look at a road, a hallway, or a row of books. Notice how parallel lines seem to lean toward a single point in the distance. You already know this — drawing just makes you put it on paper.
